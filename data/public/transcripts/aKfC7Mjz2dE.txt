Kia ora, nau mai harae mai. We are the Gut Cancer Foundation, the voice of digestive cancers in Aotearoa New Zealand. We provide information and resources to help you and your whānau navigate cancer. Did you know that your digestive system includes: the oesophagus, stomach, gallbladder and bile ducts, pancreas, and anus. Each plays a vital role in digesting food and fluids, processing nutrients, absorbing energy, regulating blood sugar and filtering waste. Cancer can occur in any of these organs when abnormal cells grow out of control. Let’s take a closer look at the stomach. The stomach is a hollow, muscular sack-like organ that receives and stores food from the oesophagus (food pipe). It has a sphincter (valve) at each end and is located on the upper left side of your abdomen. Its function is to store food while it is being mixed with stomach enzymes that breaks food down into a thick liquid and secretes a strong acid that kills any bacteria that entered with the food or drink. When the contents of the stomach are processed enough, they are released into the small intestine where nutrients are absorbed into the bloodstream. Stomach cancer occurs when abnormal cells in the stomach grow out of control. There are several types of stomach cancer. You can find out more about each type on our website. The symptoms of stomach cancer can be vague or mistaken for other things. If you notice anything unusual, and it’s been going on for a while, it’s a good idea to go get checked out at your GP. A checkup can be good for your peace of mind. The symptoms to look out for are: A painful or burning sensation or swelling in the abdomen. Heartburn or indigestion (dyspepsia). A sense of fullness, even after a small meal. Nausea and/or vomiting. Loss of appetite and/or weight loss. Unexplained tiredness or weakness. Low iron levels, red blood count/ anaemia. Blood in vomit. Black-coloured stools. There are some specific factors that may increase your risk of developing stomach cancer which include: A family history of stomach cancer due to genetics, or helicobacter pylori both of which are more prevalent in whānau Māori. If you have concerns about any symptoms or risks, a GP will be happy to see you. If you or one of your whānau have received a diagnosis of stomach cancer, you can find heaps of information, including treatment options and access to support on our website.
